Summary

The main topic was the report of the Safety working group on skull transmission and derating approaches, led by Jean-Francois Aubry. An informed approach to TUS intensity derating can help us to (conservatively) assess the safety of TUS protocols, based on stimulation and transducer parameters alone, even when no information about the individual is available.

Presentation Safety working group: Recommendations on Mechanical safety

Jean-Francois Aubry: This is part 2 of the recommendations on Mechanical safety. A briefly reminder of where we stood 6 months ago: Only physical safety, not neurophysiological at all so far.

What we discussed 6 months ago

How to translate TUS to clinics in a safe way, with a reasonable timeline? What was proposed: By building on existing guidelines. What are the closest guidelines? Imaging guidelines. Back then, we discussed mechanical and thermal safety.
Concerning mechanical safety, we discussed the mechanical index (MI). First concern was, even though we are not using very short input -just one period-, we believe it is safe to assume that we could use this mechanical index to assist the mechanical safety. In order to use this for TUS, we can first start measuring the MI in water (no skull) with the MI formula. If MI below 1.9 in water, it means that it will be even further reduced by the skull bone and it should be safe to use your set-up. Maybe in the future, we can come up with a simple analytical model that takes into account the two bone/tissue interfaces and bone attenuation. And that we might also have soon a numerical model built based on simulations. We are looking forward to models proposed by the modeling group.
6 months ago, I was not sure if the Safety group could come up with a consensus and simple analytical model. But we made progress, and we would like to share with you a draft on how to estimate the maximum intensity transmitted through the skull.

Estimation of the maximum transmission through a simple skull model

We wanted a skull model, as simple and realistic as possible. We proposed 3 layer model with absorption including skin, skull and brain. In this model, we take into account the absorption inside the skull, the attenuation by the thickness of the skull and reflection waves. You end up with a complex transmission, but this is a simple case scenario in which we assume that the skull has a given thickness, and depending on that the transmission coefficient will vary.

Transmission coefficient as a function of US frequency and skull thickness

There are currently some analytical solutions to this problem (Cobbold, 2006), but just assuming of one way incoming to the plate, then two waves in two directions, and one wave coming out the plate. Assuming no absorption, they provided a formula that gives you the transmission in energy. It also depends on the thickness of the plate, frequency, and wavelengths. If we would assume no absorption, do the calculations, we would yield the same formula as Cobbold's one (2006). This validates our approach.
We used data from Duck (1990), White et al. (2006), Robertson et al. (2017) and Leung et al. (2019) for the density, sound speed, impedance, and attenuatino for skin, skull, and brain. Many teams have been so far modelling the attenuation in the skull bone. From all models, if we take the minimum absorption by the most densed part of the cortical bone, we end up with attenuation of 83 Np/m/MHz. 83 is really the lowest one we have seen in the literature and this is also found by White et al. (2006), Robertson et al. (2017), and Leung et al. (2019). The worst case scenario would be with the smallest attenuation value.
The transmission coefficient is modelled as a function of ultrasound stimulation frequency and skull thickness. If the skull is very thick and the US frequency is high, the transmission is quite low. It increases when either the frequency or skull thickness is decreased. There are some peaks when the thickness of the bone is exactly multiple of wavelenghts you are using. Mostly, the thickness of the skull is larger than 1 mm. The perfect matching between thickness and US frequency does not happen at the time in the skull. This was the very first step: trying to find a model to estimate the transmission through the skull.

Thickness computation

We know that the skull thickness varies depending on the part of the skull you focus on. We used interpolated CT scans and calculated the thickness of the bone. Thinner bone close to temporal. Highly increasing close to frontal. This is what we have been debating a lot in the Safety group: How to take this into account. Most of the time, we use large beams that average the effect of the skull of a given surface. Depending on the size of the transducer you are using to neuromodulate, you might either average the impact of th'e skull or the very sensitive thickness variations.
Pressure transmission depends on the location and frequency. Low frequency, US transmission higher for high US frequencies. There is also a high geometric variation. With several diameters of the transducers, we averaged the transmission of the diameter of the beam that is intersected by the skull. Here, we see again the impact of the size of the transducer and US frequency.

Results

The endpoint of our small study is to provide a table for everyone of an estimation of maximum US pressure transmission calculated for 95% of all skull points (20 skulls, more than 1.4M points). For example, if you use a 200 kHz transducer with a 10 mm diameter, the pressure transmission would be close to 77%.
We did this for 20 skulls. When we look at the variation between 95% of each skull, we can see that they are pretty similar in terms of impact. Skulls vary of course in thickness, but when we look at the average and standard deviation of maximum transmission for all those 20 skulls, SD is not that big.
Maximum transmission for all skulls for all 95% of all measured skull points versus Average of maximum transmission for each skull: Pretty consistent. Compare the maximum transmission with actual measurements. In the literature, we looked at studies that had the following data: measured transmission coefficient, US beam dimater, and US frequency (White et al., 2016; Marsoc et al., 2013; Riis et al., 2021; Gimeno et al., 2019). Again, they are expected to be lower than our calculation, as we calculated the maximum transmission; this was the case. The trend is consistent, the values are higher for low frequencies and higher for smaller US beam diameter. It makes sense.
We want to provide you realistic but conservative transmission

Worst case scenario:

  • * US waves are normal to the skull
  • * Homogenous skull
  • * Lowest attenuation in skull

Limits of this first study:

  • * Only 20 skulls
  • * Only a few experimental dataset to compare
  • * Second step: hundreds of skull from all ages, genders, ...?

We would like to submit the first paper alltogether at least for the method and for the first results and then collect more data in order to make this available for future Safety groups that will provide international recommendations.
Thank you for your attention. I would like to thank the Safety group again. This was clearly a team effort. It took us some time, I, of course, did not describe all models we developed. We started with no absorption, later included absorption, then took into account that US frequency would play a role. This is the last model that we built for which we had a consensus. We are happy to discuss the model and table.
